when pitaya was discoverd?

Pitaya (Family Cactaceae, Hylocereus undatus Haw.) Pitaya or the dragon fruit is believed to be an origin of Mexico and Central and South America. Today, this fruit is widely cultivated and has gained popularity in Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia, China, Vietnam, Taiwan, Bangladesh, and Australia. Typical to a cactus plant, pitaya is an evergreen annual plant with cladode (as stem). Fruits are categorized with the presence of unique bracts on the thin skin of bright red (pink to purple)-colored fruits. Flowers and fruits of the plant are edible. Depending on the cultivar, the edible pulp portion can be either red (pink) (H. polyrhizus), white (H. undatus), or yellow (H. megalanthus) with small tiny black edible seeds. Overall, average weight of a fruit is approximately 300–350 g, and a single plant is estimated to produce up to six cycles of fruits annually. Pitaya fruit is preferred by consumers owing to their unique taste, shape, and flesh color. Though pitaya is preferred to be eaten directly, intake as fresh juice is more popular in the growing regions. Pitaya-based products like jams and candy are commercially available in Malaysia and Indonesia. Wine preparation from red pitaya is also reported in Guangxi province of China. Pitaya fruit (both skin and flesh portions) is reported to contain water-soluble betacyanins, which are responsible for the intense red (dark pink/purple) color and its antioxidant activities. In addition, lycopene, vitamin E, and beta-carotene are reported in the pulp portion. Fruits are high in total polyphenols, tannins, betalains, nonbetalainic compounds, and antioxidant compounds. Oligosaccharides from pitaya fruit are linked with prebiotic properties. Pitaya peel contains high amount of pectin and insoluble to soluble fiber. In addition, powder prepared from the fruit has potential to be used as natural food colorant. Significant amount of betacyanin pigment is reported from peel. Inhibition in the growth of melanoma cells is also reported from peel extracts of red pitaya. Intake of pitaya fruit is also linked with the reduction of dyslipidemia and promotion of wound healing capacity in diabetic rats. Seeds encompass essential fatty acids dominated by linoleic and linolenic acids.
